📜 Setting Reference: 19th Century British Mourning Customs & Rituals

In Victorian England, mourning was a crucial ritual. For the bereaved, it was a way to demonstrate the "depth of their love" to society.
1. Spatial Direction: Turning the Parlour into a Sanctuary

During this era, the home (specifically the parlour), not a funeral home, was the center of prayer and mourning.

    Mirror Veiling:

        All mirrors in the house are covered with black crepe (a thin, non-reflective fabric).

        Purpose: To prevent the soul of the deceased from being trapped in the mirror, and to spare the bereaved from seeing their own grief-stricken faces.

    The Muffled Knocker:

        Door knockers are wrapped in black cloth or thick leather to dampen the sound.

        Effect: This signifies that the household is in silence, cutting off noise from the outside world.

    Lilies & Frankincense:

        Strongly scented white lilies are displayed to mask the scent of death, and Frankincense (often used in churches) is burned inside the home.

        Trivia: The mingling of "scents of daily life" (like soup) with the "religious aroma" of frankincense is a hallmark of a Victorian mourning household.

2. Accessories: Wearable "Tokens of Love"

During the mourning period, flamboyant jewelry was forbidden. Instead, specific "Mourning Jewelry" was used.

    Jet (Black Gemstone):

        A pitch-black gemstone made of fossilized coal.

        Characteristics: It does not reflect light and is surprisingly lightweight, making it the only acceptable adornment for heavy mourning clothes.

    Hair Jewelry:

        Jewelry (brooches or lockets) containing the finely woven hair of the deceased.

        Significance: For Victorians, wearing a piece of the body was the ultimate symbol of an "eternal bond."

    Lachrymatory (Tear Bottle):

        Small glass bottles used to collect tears shed during grief.

        Tradition: Sometimes, the end of the mourning period was marked when the tears in the bottle finally evaporated.

3. Correspondence: Mourning Stationery

Special stationery was used for death announcements or replies from a mourning household.

    Black Edge (Black Border):

        The edges of the letter paper and envelopes are painted black, ranging from a few millimeters to a centimeter in thickness.

        Trivia: There was a "cruel yet delicate" rule where the border was thickest immediately after the death and became thinner over time as the grief supposedly faded.

4. Creative Guide for Writers & Artists (Tips)
Category	Descriptive Points	Character Impact
Smell	The heavy, sweet scent of Frankincense.	Makes characters intuitively feel the "closeness of death."
Visual	Mirrors covered in black cloth, wilting white lilies.	Conveys a sense of "frozen time" or household desolation.
Touch	The lightness and warmth of Jet jewelry.	During a hug, the sensation of the jewelry serves as a sudden reminder of "mourning."
Sound	Straw-covered streets, muffled door knockers.	An eerie silence that makes character voices stand out.
Summary: Application for Scene 37

The "gentle yet suffocating atmosphere" felt by Yumina and Jade in Clara's house stems from these meticulous, historically accurate rituals.

The scars of the market terror have not disappeared. Yet, they continue to live—making soup, burning incense, and veiling mirrors. This accumulation of daily life gives depth to Clara's smile and creates a sharp contrast with Iris's loneliness.
